What I did here

Design work spans computer chassis, AIO liquid cooling systems, CPU air coolers, and computer fans, taking products from concept to production while collaborating directly with engineers and manufacturers around five core principles:

  • Compatibility
  • Cost efficiency
  • Aesthetics

Overview: a flagship product, built end to end

The SK620 and SK700 were designed for Sudokoo, the company brand this project was built under. The work ran across roughly seven months, covering the full process end to end: initial draft concepts, pain point research, form and engineering development, design for manufacturing, and liaising directly with engineers in China to bring the design to production. It is the first flagship product designed from the ground up that has since been released to market.

Research: what actually goes wrong when building a PC

Before any form was drawn, the research looked at where people actually get stuck while installing a CPU cooler, not just what a finished cooler should look like.

Mounting brackets are fiddly

Fitting a heatsink and fan around a motherboard's mounting bracket is a multi step, fine motor task, tightening spring loaded screws in the right order, engaging wire clips under tension, all while working blind around a tower of fins. Small misalignments here are one of the most common points where a build stalls.

RAM clearance is a physical blocker

Tall air coolers routinely collide with tall RAM heatsinks, especially on the fin closest to the socket. Builders often discover this only after the cooler is already seated, forcing them to remove the fan, swap RAM slots, or abandon their preferred sticks entirely.

The design: a geometric cooler with a screen of its own

The Sudokoo SK700 and SK620 are high performance air coolers built for superior thermal efficiency and ultra quiet operation. The SK620 reimagines CPU cooling with a bold, geometric dual tower design, while the SK700 carries the same design language into a single tower form. Both share a sleek full black or white finish and a distinctive digital screen that displays real time system stats.

The SK700, single tower, in black and white.

The SK620, dual tower, in black and white.

Design details: angular surfaces, built for the hand as much as the eye

The SK700 and SK620 feature angular, mecha inspired surfaces that reflect ambient light for a dynamic, modern look that complements today's custom PC builds. Both models include ergonomic features such as recessed grip zones for easy fan removal and installation.

Engineering features: solving mounting and RAM clearance at the source

The two pain points identified in research, fiddly mounting and blocked RAM, were designed against directly rather than left as tradeoffs.

Rail lock fan mount

The innovative rail lock fan mount allows tool free, height adjustable installation, enhancing both usability and RAM compatibility. Instead of wrestling with wire clips, the fan slides into position along a rail and locks in place, with height adjustment built in to clear taller RAM heatsinks.

Side vents on the SK620

On the SK620, side vents are added to enhance air inflow to the central fan, improving thermal performance.

Engineering deliverables

Design intent was communicated to the engineering team directly on the CAD model, not just in renders. Annotated Rhino geometry called out exactly which surfaces needed to align or be trimmed for clearance, and side by side revision comparisons tracked how a flagged issue moved from one design pass to the next, dated and labeled so nothing was lost between mechanical design and industrial design.

Design feedback communicated directly on the CAD model, tracked from a flagged issue to the revision it produced days later.

Recognition: on the show floor at Computex and CES

Sudokoo presented the SK620 and SK700 at Computex 2025 and CES 2026, with the design personally presented on the show floor at both events.

Exploring what comes after SK620

Beyond the shipped product, internal explorations looked at forward looking directions for the next generation of fan coolers, testing more angular, faceted forms, alternate shroud proportions, and, in a further set of directions, chassis mounted screens large enough to show more than system stats.

A key consideration throughout the design process was ensuring that each product integrates seamlessly within the broader PC ecosystem. Since CPU coolers are often positioned alongside graphics cards, the visual language of the cooler should complement the overall system rather than exist as a standalone product.

Overview: a screen, not just a shroud

The LM series brings a fresh update to liquid cooling with a compact 2.4 inch IPS display that now supports MP4 video playback for a more dynamic screen experience. Work on LM360 ran across five months, led from the earliest design direction through to design for manufacturing.

LM360 is available on the DeepCool company website: view the product page →

Built for clarity

A 320 x 240 resolution ensures crisp visuals, while enhanced color accuracy and screen stability deliver consistent clarity throughout. The soft perimeter lighting adds structured contrast to the minimal design.

The power source effect: making the pump feel like the source

Beyond the screen itself, the lighting was treated as part of the product's core identity rather than decoration added on top. At the heart of the design is a softly illuminated core that appears to radiate energy outward through the layered structure. This lighting effect creates the impression that the pump is the power source of the cooling system, giving the product a sense of motion and vitality while maintaining a clean, sophisticated aesthetic.

Concept generation: from an ambitious first pass to something buildable

The following concept represents the initial design direction for the LM360 series. The original proposal explored a more premium and futuristic aesthetic, featuring layered geometries and intricate surface details that created a bold visual identity and a strong sense of depth. However, to better align with the target market, the concept was simplified to reduce production costs, improve manufacturability, and streamline assembly while preserving the core design language. This iterative process demonstrates the balance between ambitious design vision and the practical realities of mass production.

Overview: designing a brand's language into someone else's product

This project was developed for Sparkle as an OEM design assignment. The deliverable was the industrial design of a USB hub adapter, creating a solution that reflected the client's brand language while considering usability, manufacturability, and product aesthetics.

Ports as a design language

Even functional details like the port layout were treated as part of the product's visual identity, with a full width HDMI, USB-C, and ventilation array laid out to read as a single considered surface rather than a row of components bolted on afterward. The port layout also had to flex across two different types of models built for this assignment.

Two different types of models built from the same design language: the HUB 850, focused on audio and video passthrough, and the HUB 770, focused on high speed data and dual HDMI output.

CMF communicated for manufacturing

Color, material, and finish requirements were documented and handed directly to the manufacturing team, ensuring the brushed aluminum finish and assembly requirements translated accurately from design intent into a produced part.

Overview: exploration of Sudokoo's future AIO identity

This is an exploration of concepts designed for the possible directions Sudokoo's design language could lean toward. These represent work with potential for future development, or a direction the brand could follow going forward.

Borrowing from consumer audio, not other coolers

This concept explored semi translucent materials as a new visual direction for future AIO products, inspired by the growing trend of translucent speaker and audio products rather than by other cooling hardware. The design investigates how partially transparent plastics can reveal internal structure while maintaining a clean, premium appearance.

A semi translucent housing concept, letting internal structure read through the shell itself.

Reference points from consumer audio

The moodboard for this direction was pulled directly from translucent speaker design rather than other cooling hardware, including Harman Kardon's frosted, dome topped speaker enclosures, referenced here as third party inspiration rather than original design work.

A distinct brand language, beyond translucency

Another way of defining Sudokoo's design language was to create concepts that were visually distinctive and unlike existing products on the market, while maintaining a strong sense of aesthetic refinement and brand appeal.

Designer: David Ng

Overview: a pump display that adapts to the build around it

This concept explored a more adaptable AIO pump display, addressing one of the common challenges faced during PC assembly: component clearance and display orientation. The design investigates a modular screen mechanism capable of rotating 360 degrees and pivoting 90 degrees between landscape and portrait, with the display also able to shift laterally to avoid interference with nearby components such as memory modules or graphics cards.

Validated through rapid prototyping

The pump mechanism underwent multiple rounds of 3D printed prototyping to validate its movement, durability, and ease of assembly, arriving at a final mechanism that rotates, pivots, and shifts while staying compact enough to integrate into the pump housing.

Designer: David Ng

Overview: designing the part of an AIO everyone ignores

Conventional AIO designs focus almost entirely on the pump, treating the radiator as a purely functional component with minimal design consideration. This concept introduces a radiator fan cover as a new design initiative, concealing exposed mounting hardware and structural elements while extending the product's design language across the entire cooling system rather than stopping at the pump.

The full DeepCool radiator, fan array, and pump kit the cover concept was built around.

Designed against a real DeepCool radiator

The cover was not designed in isolation, it was modeled directly against a production DeepCool 360mm AIO radiator and fan array, with an exploded structure showing how a translucent fan cover would sit over the existing fan blades and radiator fins without changing the cooling hardware underneath.

The fan cover conceals exposed mounting hardware and structural elements, creating a cleaner, more cohesive appearance while extending the product's design language across the entire cooling system.

Four lighting and mounting variations

Beyond the final direction, four variants were laid out side by side for review: A1 in plain black with a subtle edge lit rim, A2 with bold gradient RGB rings covering the full face of each fan, A3 highlighting the exposed mounting bracket structure the cover is designed to conceal, and A4 in plain black with a cooler toned blue rim light.

An exploded view of the assembly, breaking the cover down into its construction layers.

The construction detail behind the cover: recessed surfaces, a decal covered panel, and the perforated venting pattern.

Proven through a working prototype

To validate the concept beyond a render, a functional prototype was fabricated using 3D printing, with LED lighting integrated into the enclosure to accurately simulate the intended visual effect, then photographed both assembled and taken apart to show how the cover shell separates from the fan and radiator assembly.

Overview: how compact can a chassis get without feeling cheap

This concept explores the design of an ultra thin mini PC chassis, pushing the boundaries of compactness while maintaining a clean, minimalist aesthetic. The objective was a form factor that minimizes its physical footprint without compromising usability or the visual refinement expected of a premium desktop product.

The orange side panel conceals a dedicated cable management compartment, allowing cables to be routed and organized neatly behind the main structure.

By combining functional venting with purely aesthetic surface detailing, the design creates a cohesive visual language where performance and form are seamlessly integrated.

This approach reinforces the minimalist identity of the chassis while ensuring that every design element contributes either functionally, aesthetically, or both.

What this body of work represents

Taken together, this portfolio represents two flagship products carried from concept to mass production, a client brand translated faithfully into an OEM product, and a continuous line of internal exploration that never stopped once a product shipped. The archive concepts, an adaptive display mechanism, a translucent material direction, a radiator treated as seriously as the pump, and a chassis platform built for future showcases, represent the design thinking that shapes what Sudokoo and DeepCool release next, even when a given concept never reaches market itself.
